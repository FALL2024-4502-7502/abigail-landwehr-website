Eight ghost towns in Wyoming worth the drive

“Isn’t all of Wyoming a ghost town?” Ha, funny. No.

The state is still pretty new in terms of state history, but territorially, it’s got a ton of it. Especially when it comes to the frontier and settlers’ past. There are plenty of relics and historic remains left scattered across the Wildwest.

 

Kane

Kane, WY 82431
44.843583, -108.202864
(307) 250-4912

Visit National Park Service Website

A tiny town left underwater after the completion of the Yellowtail Dam. Knowing that a fully filled Bighorn Lake would flood Kane, the Bureau of Reclamation bought out all of the residents to encourage them to move away. Sure enough, in 1967, the lake's rising waters drowned out Kane for good.

Atlantic City

Atlantic City, WY 82520
42.496455, -108.730549
(307) 250-4912

Visit Bureau of Land Management's website

Now reclaimed by the state’s wildlife, the former gold mining camp used to see the constant passing through of miners and travelers in the 1800s. About 150,000 pioneers went west over four years in the 1800s. When mining opportunities began to disappear, the town’s population followed.

Gebo

Gebo, WY 82443
43.793617, -108.227269
(307) 250-4912

Visit Bureau of Land Management's website

Sharing a past of mining, the town was first established in 1907 as a coal town. Over 2,000 people lived in the town but when mining started to slow, the population dwindled too. The remains of the town were bulldozed in 1971, though some buildings and the cemetery remain.

Kirwin

Kirwin, WY 82433
43.876318, -109.297892
(307) 250-4912

Visit Forest Service's website

At its peak, approximately 200 miners and their families lived in Kirwin. The town came to life in the mid-1880s after gold and silver were discovered on Spar Mountain. However, in 1907, an avalanche from a nearby mountain destroyed several buildings and killed three people. It pushed many families to leave in the spring and led to the overall deserting of the town.

Sunrise

Sunrise, WY 82215
42.329995, -104.705530
(307) 250-4912

Visit Wyoming State Historic Preservation Office's website

Sunrise is unique in how it’s been preserved. The town started in the early 1900s, growing to employ over 547 in the nearby mines. When the steel market saw a decline in the 1980s, so did the town. It’s rare to see mining towns intact as this ghost town, holding its ground with multiple still-standing buildings.

Piedmont

Piedmont, WY 82930
41.215997, -110.627394
(307) 250-4912

Visit Wyoming Park's website

Piedmont was a small, active railroad and timber town founded in 1867. It thrived for a time, even building large kilns to make charcoal for railroad ties. However, the town was later neglected when the Union Pacific line was built and bypassed the area. Ultimately, the very reason for the town's establishment contributed to its downfall.

Eadsville

Eadsville, WY 82604
42.729272, -106.360751
(307) 250-4912

Visit the Historical Marker Database's website

Eadsville is a ghost town located 12 miles south of Casper on Casper Mountain. Founded in 1891, it grew around a large spring after news of gold, silver, lead, and copper deposits spread. Lots were sold, and about a dozen cabins were built, with three foundations still standing today. The town was abandoned by 1905-1906, though asbestos and feldspar were mined, with feldspar still being extracted today.

Arland

Arland, WY 82433
44.190900, -108.871400
(307) 250-4912

Visit the Meeteetse Visitor Center's website

In terms of history, the ghost town of Arland has the darkest past. It began as a mining town in the 1800s but fell into decline less than a century later. The town's story involves a love triangle and a series of brutal murders rooted in a cycle of vengeance. Even though the graves have since been moved 32 miles away, the ghost town is infamous for causing disturbances, such as hearing voices and experiencing electrical problems in vehicles that pass by.
